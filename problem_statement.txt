Problem Overview 
Every day, thousands of hardware parts travel across a global network of hubs, satellite 
locations, and repair centers. When a part fails at a customer site, it needs to travel forward — 
from a warehouse to the customer. When a part is broken and needs repair, it travels in reverse 
— from the customer back through the network to a repair center, and once fixed, onward to be 
redeployed. 
This network is expensive and complex. Parts are often routed inefficiently — sent from a 
distant hub when a closer satellite had stock, or allowed to sit at a congested hub when a faster 
route was available. SLA commitments are missed, logistics costs balloon, and customers wait 
longer than necessary. 
Your challenge is to build an Agentic AI system, powered by geospatial analysis and historical 
logistics data, that autonomously analyses routing patterns, identifies inefficiencies, optimizes 
routing decisions, and produces visual and actionable insights — for both forward and reverse 
logistics flows. 
You are given a dataset of 1,800 real-world-scale logistics transactions across 12 hub locations, 
8 repair centers, and 178 unique parts, with a total logistics cost pool of USD 65 million. Your 
system must make sense of this data and help decision-makers cut costs, reduce delays, and 
keep repair inventory healthy.

The Core Optimization Problem 
Right now, routing decisions are often made manually or based on simple rules (e.g., always use 
the nearest hub). 
Your AI agent must learn from 1,800 historical transactions — analysing cost, SLA performance, 
hub utilisation, and transit times — to recommend smarter routing decisions. 
The goal: reduce total logistics cost and SLA breach rates by at least 15–20% compared to the 
current routing patterns visible in the dataset.

Core Challenges to Solve 
Challenge 1 — Geospatial Network Visualisation 
Before optimising anything, you need to see the network. Build an interactive geospatial map 
that visualises: 
• All hub locations plotted on a map with type indicators (Primary / Regional / Satellite / 
International) 
• All TPR locations plotted with current workload and capacity indicators 
• Active shipment routes as lines connecting origin → intermediate hub → destination 
(colour-coded by flow type and health status) 
• A heat-map or bubble chart showing where SLA breaches and tamper alerts are 
concentrated geographically 
• A choropleth or flow diagram showing total logistics cost by origin-destination corridor 
 
Deliverable: An interactive map (use Folium, Plotly, Kepler.gl, Deck.gl, or any mapping library) 
that a logistics manager can use to understand the network at a glance. 
 
Challenge 2 — Route Efficiency Analysis 
Analyse historical routing patterns to identify where inefficiencies exist: 
• Identify the top 10 most expensive routing corridors (origin-hub to destination) and 
explain why they are expensive 
• Detect routes where parts were sourced from a distant hub even though a closer hub or 
satellite had sufficient stock 
• Calculate the average cost per unit per km for each corridor and identify outliers 
• Identify which hubs are consistently over-utilised (high-cost routing bottlenecks) vs 
under-utilised 
• Compare actual transit days vs expected for each corridor and flag chronic 
underperformers 
 
Deliverable: A visual dashboard showing corridor efficiency rankings, cost-per-km analysis, and 
hub utilisation heatmap. 
 
Challenge 3 — Agentic Routing Recommender 
Build an AI agent that, given a new part request, recommends the optimal routing path. The 
agent must: 
• Accept inputs: Part No, Quantity, Priority, Destination City 
• Query the Hub Location Master and current stock levels to determine which hubs have 
available stock 
• Apply a routing decision tree: Nearest Hub with Stock → Nearest Satellite → Next Hub 
→ International Source 
• Calculate the estimated cost and transit time for each candidate route 
• Recommend the best route with a justification (cost, distance, availability, SLA 
confidence) 
• Generate an alternative route as a backup if the primary route has risk (low stock, 
overloaded hub) 
• Learn from historical SLA breach patterns: if a route has a 40%+ breach history, warn 
the user and downrank it 
 
Deliverable: A working agent (demo via CLI, Streamlit, or Gradio) that accepts a routing request 
and outputs a ranked recommendation with cost estimates and risk flags. 
 
Challenge 4 — Cost Optimisation Model 
Your agent should be able to answer: "How much could we save if we routed smarter?" 
• Identify all transactions in the dataset where a suboptimal route was used (i.e., a 
cheaper alternative was available) 
• Calculate the excess cost of the suboptimal routing for each such transaction 
• Aggregate potential savings by hub, by part category, by logistics partner, and by flow 
type 
• Model the impact of holding more inventory at satellite locations (reducing the need for 
expensive inter-hub transfers) 
• Produce a "What-If" analysis: if the top-3 most expensive corridors were re-routed 
optimally, what is the total saving? 
 
Deliverable: A cost optimisation report (visual + tabular) showing current vs optimised cost per 
corridor, total potential savings, and inventory investment needed to unlock those savings. 
 
Challenge 5 — Reverse Logistics Optimisation 
Optimising the reverse flow (broken part collection → repair → redeployment) is equally 
important: 
• Analyse which repair centers (TPRs) are over-capacity and which are underutilised 
• Identify broken parts that were routed to a distant TPR when a closer one with capacity 
and specialisation match was available 
• Calculate the cost of redeployment: once a part is repaired, is it being sent back to the 
hub with the highest demand for that part? 
• Detect repair consolidation opportunities: multiple small single-part shipments from the 
same hub to the same TPR that could be batched 
• Flag TPRs where current stock of repair inputs (spare components) is below minimum 
threshold — generate a restock alert 
 
Deliverable: A reverse logistics optimisation dashboard with TPR utilisation view, consolidation 
opportunities, redeployment efficiency analysis, and restock alert list. 
 
Challenge 6 — SLA Breach Predictor 
Train a lightweight predictive model to predict whether a shipment will breach its SLA before it is 
dispatched: 
• Features to consider: origin hub, destination, part category, priority, quantity, logistics 
partner, departure day of week, current hub utilisation, stock level at origin 
• Target variable: SLA_Breach (YES / NO) from the Logistics_Transactions sheet 
• Build a simple classifier (Decision Tree, Random Forest, or Logistic Regression — 
accessible to 2nd year students) 
• Evaluate performance: accuracy, precision, recall, confusion matrix 
• Integrate the predictor into the routing recommender: if a recommended route has a 
predicted SLA breach probability > 50%, flag it 
 
Deliverable: A trained model with evaluation metrics and integration into the routing agent as a 
risk signal.

Security & Privacy Considerations 
Even at prototype level, keep these principles in mind: 
• Treat location coordinates and shipment volumes as sensitive operational data — do not 
expose them in public URLs or logs 
• Parameterise all thresholds (SLA days, cost limits, stock minimums) — never hard-code 
business rules 
• Maintain an audit trail of every recommendation your agent makes, with the data and 
rules used as evidence 
• If integrating with external APIs (mapping, LLMs), ensure API keys are stored in 
environment variables, never in source code
